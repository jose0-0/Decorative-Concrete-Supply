Commercial concrete work is a different animal from residential. The scope is larger, the timelines are compressed, the number of people involved in the decision-making is greater, and the consequences of getting things wrong are more significant. A missed deadline on a residential patio is inconvenient. A missed deadline on a commercial build-out that's holding up a tenant's opening is a legal and financial problem.

Contractors who do residential work well don't automatically do commercial work well. The skills overlap but the planning discipline required for commercial projects is more rigorous than what residential work demands. This is the third pillar article in this series, written for contractors who are doing commercial concrete work or considering moving into it. It's a framework for thinking through the full scope of a project from initial engagement through final acceptance.

## Understanding the Commercial Project Environment

Before getting into the planning specifics, it helps to understand how commercial projects are different from the ground up.

**Multiple decision-makers.** On a residential project, you're typically dealing with one or two homeowners who make all the decisions. On a commercial project, you may be dealing with a general contractor, an architect, an owner's representative, a facilities manager, and the end user of the space. Each of these parties has different priorities and different levels of authority. Understanding who makes what decisions and whose approval is required before work proceeds is part of the job.

**Contract documents and specifications.** Commercial projects typically involve formal contract documents — drawings, specifications, and contract language — that define the work more precisely than a residential proposal does. The specification for the concrete work describes the product systems, application standards, testing requirements, and performance criteria that the finished work needs to meet. Reading and understanding the specification before you bid is not optional.

**Submittal requirements.** Many commercial projects require contractors to submit product data sheets, samples, and method statements for approval before work begins. The submittal process takes time and needs to be initiated early. Discovering that a submittal hasn't been approved after you've already ordered materials is a problem.

**Inspection and documentation.** Commercial work often involves inspections, testing, and documentation requirements that residential work doesn't. Knowing what's required and building it into the project plan prevents delays when inspectors show up at stages you weren't expecting.

**Schedule coordination.** Commercial projects involve multiple trades working in sequence and sometimes simultaneously. Your work schedule needs to be coordinated with the general contractor's master schedule. Showing up to do floor work when another trade hasn't finished what needs to happen before you can start is lost time that affects everyone.

## Phase 1 — Pre-Bid and Scope Development

The planning work that happens before you submit a bid determines whether you win a profitable job or a losing one.

**Read the full specification.** For concrete and flooring work, the specification section describes the required product systems, surface preparation standards, application methods, and performance requirements. It may specify products by manufacturer and model number, or it may specify performance requirements and allow you to propose equivalent products. Understanding exactly what's required before you price it is the only way to price it correctly.

**Walk the site before bidding.** On a commercial project, the existing conditions — substrate condition, access constraints, adjacent finished work that needs protection, utilities and penetrations in the floor — directly affect the scope and cost of the work. A site visit before bidding is as important on a commercial job as on a residential one and often more so given the scale of the work.

**Clarify scope boundaries.** What exactly is included in your scope and what isn't? Where does the floor work start and stop relative to other trades? Who is responsible for protection of your finished work after you complete it? Who handles concrete repair if the substrate condition is worse than expected? These questions are better resolved before bidding than through change orders after the fact.

**Review the schedule.** When is the work expected to start? What's the completion deadline? Are there intermediate milestone dates that affect when specific areas need to be complete? How does your work sequence relate to other trades who need to complete work before or after you? Understanding the schedule constraints before bidding allows you to staff and sequence the work correctly.

## Phase 2 — Pre-Construction Planning

After award, the pre-construction phase is where the detailed planning that makes the project run smoothly happens.

**Submittals.** Prepare and submit all required product data sheets, sample panels, and method statements as early as possible. The review and approval process takes time and the work can't start in most cases until submittals are approved. A submittal submitted the week before the scheduled start date with a 10-day review period is a schedule problem.

**Sample panels.** Many commercial decorative concrete specifications require an approved sample panel that establishes the acceptable appearance standard for the finished work. The sample panel needs to be made with the actual products and techniques that will be used on the project. It's reviewed and approved before production work starts and serves as the reference for acceptance of the finished work.

Take this seriously. A sample panel that's made quickly and not representative of what you can actually produce creates a problem when the finished work doesn't match it. A sample panel that demonstrates your best work creates a standard you can meet and a reference that protects you when subjective appearance questions come up during acceptance.

**Material procurement.** Commercial projects often involve larger quantities of specialty products than a typical supply house stocks. Confirm availability of everything you need before the project starts, not the week before installation begins. Lead times for specialty products can be several weeks, and a material shortage that delays a commercial project has cost consequences that affect the entire project team.

**Crew planning.** What crew size does the project require to meet the schedule? Do you have the skilled personnel available or do you need to hire additional crew or subcontract specific tasks? Commercial projects often require crew sizes larger than a typical residential contractor maintains, and scaling up crew on short notice is harder than planning for it early.

**Equipment planning.** What equipment is needed and when? Is it owned or does it need to be rented? Surface preparation on commercial floors often involves larger equipment than residential work — ride-on grinders, larger shot blast machines, industrial vacuum systems. Confirm equipment availability and schedule it before the project starts.

## Phase 3 — Surface Preparation

Surface preparation on commercial concrete floors follows the same principles as residential work but at a larger scale and with more formal requirements.

The specification typically calls out a required surface profile — often expressed as a CSP (Concrete Surface Profile) level per ICRI standards. The preparation method needs to achieve the specified profile consistently across the entire work area. Shot blasting is the standard method for achieving consistent profile on large commercial floors. Diamond grinding is used for areas where shot blasting isn't practical.

Testing before and during preparation is part of commercial work. Moisture vapor emission testing, pull-off adhesion testing of primers, and other QC steps are often specified. Build the time and cost of this testing into the project plan.

Document the preparation thoroughly. Photos at each stage, test results, and records of the preparation method and equipment used create the documentation trail that protects you if questions about the work come up later.

## Phase 4 — Product Application

The application phase on a commercial project requires the same discipline as preparation but at a pace that's appropriate for the project scale.

**Work in manageable sections.** Large commercial floors are typically worked in sections rather than as a single application. Section size is governed by product working time, crew size, and the physical layout of the space. Define section boundaries before starting and make sure the crew understands the sequence.

**Maintain quality control.** On a large floor, coverage rate consistency, thickness consistency, and appearance consistency across the entire surface require active monitoring. Designate someone on the crew whose job is quality monitoring — checking coverage, watching for defects, and flagging issues before they become larger problems.

**Follow the specification.** This sounds obvious but it's where commercial work gets into trouble. The specification defines the application method, the number of coats, the dry times between coats, and the conditions under which work can proceed. Deviating from the specification — even when the deviation seems reasonable in the moment — creates liability if questions arise about the finished work.

**Document as you go.** Lot numbers of materials used, application dates and times, temperature and humidity conditions during application, any deviations from the specification with the reason and authorization. This documentation is standard practice on well-run commercial projects and protects everyone involved.

## Phase 5 — Inspection and Acceptance

Commercial project acceptance is a formal process that residential work usually isn't. Understanding what acceptance involves and preparing for it prevents surprises at the end of the project.

**Pre-final walkthrough.** Before the formal final walkthrough with the owner's representative, walk the completed work yourself with a critical eye. Address any deficiencies you identify before the formal review. Presenting work that needs obvious touch-ups to an owner's representative creates a poor impression of the overall quality of the work.

**Punch list response.** The formal walkthrough typically produces a punch list of items to be corrected. Respond to punch list items promptly and completely. Leaving punch list items open for extended periods creates tension with the project team and can affect final payment.

**Closeout documentation.** Commercial projects require closeout documentation — warranties, product data sheets, application records, test results, maintenance instructions. Prepare these documents during the project rather than trying to assemble them at closeout. A contractor who produces complete, professional closeout documentation on the first request makes a strong impression for future work.

## Building a Commercial Concrete Business

Commercial concrete work rewards contractors who are organized, disciplined, and able to execute at scale. The margins can be better than residential work for contractors who do it well, and the project size means that each successful commercial project has more impact on annual revenue than multiple smaller residential jobs.

The reputation built on commercial work also compounds. General contractors who work with a subcontractor who delivers quality work on schedule with minimal problems will call them first on the next job. That kind of relationship-based pipeline is the most stable foundation for a commercial concrete business.
