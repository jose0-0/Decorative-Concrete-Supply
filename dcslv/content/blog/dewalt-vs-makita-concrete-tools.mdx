The DeWalt versus Makita debate has been running in the trades for as long as both brands have been serious players in professional tools. Ask ten contractors which one they prefer and you'll get a mix of strong opinions, brand loyalties built over years of use, and a few people who run both without strong preference either way.

We carry both brands at DCS because the honest answer is that both are genuinely good tools and the right choice depends on what you're using them for, what platform you're already invested in, and personal preference based on how they feel in your hands. What we're not going to do is tell you one brand is definitively better in every category — that's not accurate and it's not useful.

What we can do is give you a grounded comparison of how each performs on the specific tools that concrete contractors use most, based on what we hear from the contractors who buy from us and use these tools on Las Vegas job sites every day.

## The Platform Question

Before comparing individual tools, the platform question is worth addressing because it affects the whole decision.

Both DeWalt and Makita have mature, extensive cordless platforms — DeWalt's 20V MAX and FLEXVOLT systems, and Makita's 18V LXT and 40V XGT systems. If you're already invested in one platform with multiple tools and batteries, that investment has real weight in the decision. Switching platforms means new batteries and chargers, which adds cost that may or may not be justified by the performance difference on a specific tool.

For contractors who are starting fresh or building out a tool set without existing platform investment, the platform comparison is more open. For those who already have a full DeWalt or Makita battery ecosystem, the question is usually whether a specific tool from the other brand is compelling enough to justify the battery cost.

## Angle Grinders

Angle grinders are among the most frequently used tools in concrete prep work — diamond cup wheel grinding for surface preparation, cutting wheels for crack routing, and various surface treatment applications. Both DeWalt and Makita make strong angle grinders across corded and cordless configurations.

DeWalt's angle grinder lineup covers a range of disc sizes and power levels. Their corded grinders have a reputation for durability in heavy-use applications and the motor protection features on newer models have addressed some of the overheating issues that older generations had under sustained load in hot environments.

Makita's angle grinders are widely respected in the trades and their brushless motor technology in recent generations has improved efficiency and longevity significantly. The brushless motor advantage is most relevant for sustained use — extended grinding sessions where a brushed motor would generate more heat and experience more wear.

For concrete surface preparation work — extended grinding sessions on garage floors or large flatwork areas — brushless motor technology from either brand is the right specification. Both DeWalt and Makita offer brushless angle grinders at comparable price points.

## Rotary Hammers and Demolition

Rotary hammers are used for core drilling, anchor installation, crack routing, and demolition work on concrete. Both brands have strong offerings in this category and it's one where professional concrete contractors tend to have clearer preferences.

Makita has historically been very strong in rotary hammers. Their SDS-Plus and SDS-Max rotary hammers have a long track record of performance in professional concrete work and are well-regarded in the contractor community for their vibration control and sustained performance under demanding conditions. If you talk to concrete contractors about rotary hammers specifically, Makita comes up consistently as a preferred brand.

DeWalt's rotary hammers have improved significantly in recent generations and are competitive with Makita in most applications. Their FLEXVOLT cordless rotary hammers in particular offer a cordless option with performance that approaches corded tools, which matters for job sites where cord management is a practical concern.

For applications involving significant core drilling or sustained demolition work, both brands perform well. Makita's longer track record in this specific category gives it an edge in contractor confidence, but DeWalt's current lineup is genuinely competitive.

## Drills and Mixing

Mixing concrete products requires a drill powerful enough to drive a heavy-duty paddle mixer through thick overlay material, repair mortars, and epoxy systems without bogging down or overheating. This is a different use case from standard construction drilling and it's worth evaluating separately.

Both DeWalt and Makita make high-torque drill drivers capable of driving mixing paddles through thick material. The key specifications for mixing applications are torque output, speed control, and handle ergonomics for extended mixing sessions.

For mixing applications specifically, a corded drill often outperforms cordless options because mixing through dense material can drain battery charge quickly. Both brands make capable corded drills in the relevant power range. The choice here often comes down to ergonomics and handle design rather than significant performance differences between brands.

## Circular Saws for Form Work

Circular saws are used for cutting form lumber, making template cuts, and general site work. Both DeWalt and Makita make excellent circular saws and this is one of the categories where personal preference and handle feel tend to drive contractor preference more than objective performance differences.

DeWalt's circular saws have a broad following and their cordless offerings in the FLEXVOLT platform bring corded-level performance to battery operation. Makita's circular saws are well-built and the ergonomics are preferred by a significant portion of the contractor community.

If you already have batteries in either platform, the circular saw choice is straightforward. If you're deciding without platform constraints, handling both in person before buying is worthwhile — circular saw ergonomics vary enough between brands that what feels natural to one person feels awkward to another.

## Tool Durability on Las Vegas Job Sites

Las Vegas job site conditions create specific durability demands that are worth considering in any tool comparison. Summer temperatures on an active job site are extreme. Tools stored in trucks or trailers between uses get exposed to heat that exceeds the storage temperature specifications of most electronics. Dust is constant and fine enough to penetrate anywhere that's not well sealed.

Both DeWalt and Makita have improved their dust sealing and thermal management over recent product generations in response to contractor feedback about exactly these conditions. Current-generation brushless tools from both brands handle Las Vegas summer conditions better than older brushed motor tools did.

The practical durability difference between comparable-tier tools from DeWalt and Makita in Las Vegas conditions is small for contractors who maintain their tools properly — keep them clean, store them out of sustained extreme heat when possible, and follow the maintenance guidance in the product documentation. The difference between well-maintained tools and poorly maintained tools of either brand is larger than the difference between DeWalt and Makita in the same tier.

## Service and Parts Availability

Both brands have service networks in the Las Vegas area and parts availability for current tool lines is good through authorized service centers. This matters when a tool needs repair mid-project and turnaround time affects job schedules.

For contractors who depend on a specific tool daily, having a backup tool of the same model is more practical than relying on service center turnaround regardless of brand. The service network argument is more relevant for tools that are used occasionally rather than daily.

## Price Comparison

At comparable tiers — professional grade to professional grade — DeWalt and Makita are priced similarly. Both brands have professional-grade lines at premium price points and more economical lines at lower price points. Comparing the professional-grade DeWalt tool to the economical Makita equivalent isn't an apples-to-apples comparison.

When evaluating price, compare tools at the same tier within each brand rather than the lowest available price from each. A professional concrete contractor whose tools take daily abuse is better served by professional-grade tools that last than by economical alternatives that need replacing more frequently.

Kit pricing — tools bundled with batteries and chargers — is worth evaluating for contractors starting a new platform. Both brands offer kit packages that reduce the per-tool cost when multiple tools and batteries are purchased together.

## The Bottom Line

For concrete work specifically, both DeWalt and Makita produce tools that professional contractors trust and use successfully on demanding job sites. The decision comes down to platform investment, personal ergonomic preference, and in a few specific categories — rotary hammers in particular — the track record that has built stronger contractor preference for one brand over the other.

If you're currently in one platform and happy with it, there's no compelling reason to switch based on this comparison. If you're building a tool set fresh, handling tools from both brands before committing is the most useful research you can do.
