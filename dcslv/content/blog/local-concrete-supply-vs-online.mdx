Online ordering has changed how contractors and homeowners buy almost everything. Materials that used to require a trip to the supply house can now arrive on a job site two days after clicking a button. For commodities — fasteners, basic lumber, standard concrete products that are well understood and don't require much guidance — online ordering works fine.

Decorative concrete products are not commodities. And the assumption that they can be purchased the same way as a box of screws has produced a lot of job site problems that wouldn't have happened with a phone call to someone who knows the products and knows the Las Vegas market.

This isn't an argument against using technology or against the convenience that online purchasing provides. It's an argument for understanding what online ordering can and can't do — and why the things it can't do matter more on a concrete job site than they do when you're buying almost anything else.

## The Product Knowledge Gap

Decorative concrete involves dozens of product categories, hundreds of individual products, and an enormous number of variables around how those products interact with each other, with the substrate, and with the conditions of the specific job site.

An online product listing tells you the product name, the coverage rate, and the listed application conditions. It doesn't tell you whether that product is appropriate for the substrate you're working with. It doesn't tell you whether it's compatible with what's already on the surface. It doesn't tell you whether it will hold up in Las Vegas heat and UV conditions. And it doesn't tell you whether the contractor you're buying it for has used it before or whether there are application considerations that a first-time user needs to know.

A local supplier who knows their products can tell you all of those things before you buy anything. That conversation — which takes five to ten minutes — prevents the kind of product selection mistakes that result in failed applications, rework, and unhappy customers.

Over 30 years of supplying decorative concrete products in Las Vegas, the most expensive job site problems we've helped contractors and homeowners work through have had one thing in common: the wrong product was purchased without enough information to make the right choice. Online purchasing didn't create that problem, but it removed the natural checkpoint that a knowledgeable supplier provides.

## Local Knowledge Is Not Available Online

The Las Vegas market has specific characteristics that affect product selection in ways that general product information doesn't account for.

The UV intensity here degrades sealers and coatings faster than in moderate climates. Products that perform adequately in markets with less sun exposure need to be specifically UV-stable formulations to hold up on a Las Vegas outdoor surface. A product review from a contractor in the Pacific Northwest is not useful information for a contractor in Las Vegas because the climate conditions are fundamentally different.

The soil conditions in parts of the Las Vegas valley — expansive clays, caliche layers, fill soils from widespread residential development — affect concrete performance in ways that influence product selection for repair and resurfacing work. A local supplier who has watched projects succeed and fail in specific neighborhoods over many years has practical knowledge that no product data sheet captures.

The ready-mix plants, the concrete characteristics of pours in different parts of the valley, the subcontractors and crews that work in this market — knowing the ecosystem that a product is being used in is part of being a useful supplier. That knowledge doesn't exist in an online transaction.

## The Problem of Counterfeit and Substandard Products

The online marketplace for construction products has a counterfeit and quality problem that most buyers don't know about until a product fails to perform the way it should.

Name-brand decorative concrete products — from manufacturers with established reputations — are routinely counterfeited or adulterated by third-party sellers on major online platforms. The packaging looks identical. The product inside is not. A contractor who has used a specific product successfully for years and orders it online believing they're getting the same thing may be getting something entirely different.

Substandard products that don't meet the specifications of the name on the label are another version of the same problem. The testing and quality control that established manufacturers apply to their products isn't applied to knockoffs, and the performance difference shows up on the job site.

A licensed distributor of the brands they carry receives product directly from the manufacturer through the authorized supply chain. What's on the shelf is what the manufacturer made, to the manufacturer's specifications. That assurance doesn't exist in an online marketplace where third-party sellers can list products they sourced from anywhere.

## Availability When You Need It

Decorative concrete work is time-sensitive. A pour is scheduled. The concrete is on the way. The crew is on site. Running short of a product at that point and waiting two days for an online order is not an option.

A local supply house that stocks the products you use consistently is a risk management tool. When something unexpected happens — a product runs short, a container is damaged, the substrate condition requires an additional product that wasn't originally planned — you can get what you need the same day. Sometimes the same hour.

That availability has real dollar value on an active job site. The cost of a day's delay for a full crew waiting for a product to arrive from an online order is many multiples of any savings the online price might have offered.

## Returns and Problem Resolution

When something goes wrong with a product — a sealer that doesn't cure correctly, a batch that looks different from previous batches, a container that was damaged and may have been compromised — how that gets resolved is completely different with a local supplier versus an online purchase.

With a local supplier who has a relationship with you and with the manufacturer, a problem gets addressed directly. The supplier looks at the situation, talks to their manufacturer representative, and figures out what happened and what the solution is. If there's a product defect, it gets handled.

With an online purchase through a third-party marketplace, the resolution process involves customer service systems, return policies, and time delays that don't account for the fact that you have a job site that needs to keep moving. Getting replacement product or a credit against a failed application is a long and uncertain process compared to working with a local supplier who is invested in your success.

## What Online Ordering Is Good For

To be fair about this: online ordering is genuinely useful for some things in the concrete trade. Commodity items that are well understood and where price is the primary variable. Reference materials and technical documentation. Equipment research before a purchase decision. These are all appropriate uses of online resources.

The mistake is applying the commodity purchasing model to specialty decorative concrete products where knowledge, quality assurance, and local relevance matter as much as price. Saving a few dollars per unit on a sealer that isn't appropriate for your substrate or your climate doesn't look like savings after the application fails.
