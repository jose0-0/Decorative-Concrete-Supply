December is when Las Vegas homeowners start thinking about what they want to do to their properties before summer arrives. The outdoor living season in Las Vegas essentially starts in March and runs through May before the heat makes extended outdoor time less comfortable. Projects that are done and cured well before the summer heat arrives look and perform better than ones that get rushed in late spring when the window is closing.

Planning now — while contractors have good availability and you have time to think through the project properly — is significantly better than trying to squeeze a project into a two-week window in April when everyone else has the same idea.

Here are five decorative concrete projects that are worth planning for spring, what they involve, and what you need to think through before getting quotes.

## Project 1 — Pool Deck Resurfacing

This is the single most impactful outdoor concrete project most Las Vegas homeowners can do. A fresh pool deck transforms the entire outdoor space, improves safety through better traction, and makes the pool area more enjoyable from the first warm day of spring.

The timing argument for spring is strong. Pool deck work done in March and April is executed in ideal application conditions — temperatures are cooperative, the concrete has time to cure fully before the heat arrives, and the deck is ready for heavy use when pool season starts. Pool deck work attempted in June is competing against the heat and the calendar simultaneously.

What to think through before getting quotes: What's the current condition of the deck — is it structurally sound or are there heaving, cracking, or structural issues that need professional assessment? What finish are you looking for — a practical texture coat, a stamped overlay, or something in between? Do the existing steps, raised features, or other deck elements need attention at the same time?

## Project 2 — Patio Overlay or Staining

An existing concrete patio that's worn, faded, or just plain gray can be completely transformed with an overlay or staining project. This is a high-return investment in terms of how significantly it changes the appearance and usability of an outdoor space relative to what it costs.

For patios in good structural condition with cosmetic wear, a stained and sealed surface is a reasonable spring project for an attentive homeowner or a one-day job for an experienced contractor. For patios with surface deterioration, a thin overlay resurfacing before staining and sealing produces a cleaner result.

The spring planning advantage here is primarily about contractor availability. This type of project gets in high demand as spring progresses and homeowners start acting on the outdoor improvement ideas they've been thinking about through winter. Getting on a good contractor's schedule in January for March or April execution is significantly easier than calling in April for April work.

What to think through: Does the patio need an overlay or will staining alone achieve the look you want? What aesthetic are you after — natural stone look, solid color, decorative pattern? Is the surface in sound enough condition to accept overlay or stain directly?

## Project 3 — Garage Floor Epoxy Coating

Spring is one of the best times of year for garage floor epoxy work in Las Vegas because temperature conditions are ideal for application and cure. The hot summer temperatures that make garage floor epoxy application difficult are months away, and the finished floor is fully cured and hardened before the garage starts getting hot in June.

A garage floor epoxy project done well adds a finished, professional appearance to a space that's often overlooked in home improvement planning. For homeowners who use their garage as an actual workspace, a sealed floor that's easy to clean and resistant to oil and chemical staining is a genuine quality-of-life improvement.

The planning step that matters most for garage floor epoxy is moisture testing. Before committing to a spring project, tape a plastic sheet to the floor in a few locations and leave it for 24 hours. If there's condensation on the underside of the plastic, the slab has moisture vapor transmission that needs to be addressed in the product selection and application approach. Discovering this after a contractor is already scheduled is a problem. Discovering it in December gives you time to plan for it.

What to think through: What system — standard epoxy, metallic, decorative flake? What's the current floor condition — any existing coating to remove, significant staining to address? Is moisture vapor transmission a concern based on the plastic sheet test?

## Project 4 — Driveway Resurfacing or Stamped Concrete

A driveway is one of the first things visible from the street and one of the surfaces that gets the most use. Driveways in Las Vegas take significant abuse from UV exposure, temperature cycling, and vehicle traffic, and most need attention every ten to fifteen years.

For driveways in good structural condition, a resurfacing overlay with color and sealing can look as good as a new concrete pour at considerably less cost. For driveways with significant surface deterioration or structural issues, full replacement is the honest recommendation — an overlay over a failing driveway substrate is money spent on a surface that will fail prematurely.

Stamped concrete on a new driveway installation is a spring project that rewards early planning because the forming, subgrade preparation, and concrete scheduling involved in a new pour require coordination. Getting a contractor engaged in winter for a spring pour means the project happens in the ideal window rather than being pushed into summer.

What to think through: Is the existing driveway structurally sound or does it need replacement? If replacing, what finish — plain, stamped, exposed aggregate? If resurfacing, what product system and what appearance are you after?

## Project 5 — Walkways, Entry Features, and Borders

The connecting surfaces — the walkway from the driveway to the front entry, the path around the pool, the border between the lawn and the patio — are often the last things homeowners prioritize and the first things visitors notice.

Decorative concrete borders, walkways, and entry features done well create a cohesive outdoor space where everything connects intentionally. Done poorly or neglected, they make an otherwise well-maintained property look unfinished.

Spring planning for these projects often involves coordination with other outdoor work — if the pool deck is being resurfaced, addressing the surrounding walkways and borders at the same time creates a unified appearance and avoids the visible seam between surfaces treated at different times. Thinking about the whole outdoor space rather than individual surfaces in isolation produces better results.

What to think through: What surfaces connect and is there a coherent design approach across them? Are there transitions between the decorative concrete surfaces and other materials — stone, pavers, grass — that need careful detailing? Is the timing coordinated with other outdoor work that's planned for the same spring season?

## Planning Now Pays Off in Spring

The common theme across all five of these projects is that planning in December and January for spring execution produces better outcomes than waiting until spring to start thinking about it.

Better contractor availability — the good contractors book up as spring approaches. Getting on their schedule in winter puts you ahead of the rush.

Better material planning — specialty products, color selections, and anything that requires lead time can be sorted in advance rather than creating delays when the project is ready to start.

Better project scope clarity — thinking through the project carefully over the winter months produces a clearer scope and fewer mid-project surprises than jumping into something quickly.

Better spring — a pool deck that's done and cured by the end of March is ready when the warm days start arriving. A pool deck squeezed into May is done just as summer is starting.
