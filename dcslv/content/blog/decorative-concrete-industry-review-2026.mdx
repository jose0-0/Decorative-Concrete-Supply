Every December we get a natural moment to step back and look at the year that's ending. What worked, what didn't, what surprised us, and what we're carrying into the next year. After 30-plus years of doing this, those reflections still produce something useful — the patterns that show up in a single year's worth of customer conversations, job site observations, and market activity tell you something about where things are headed.

2026 was a solid year for decorative concrete work in Las Vegas. Here is what we observed and what we think it means.

## What Drove Activity This Year

Residential demand was consistent through 2026, with pool deck resurfacing remaining one of the most active project categories in the valley. The combination of an aging housing stock in established neighborhoods and continued new construction creating homes whose owners are ready to upgrade flatwork kept contractors busy throughout most of the year.

The fall season came through as expected. The window between late September and mid-November produced a concentrated surge of outdoor project activity that tracked closely with what we've seen in previous years. Contractors who had their fall pipelines built before the season opened — who were scheduling in August for October execution — had stronger quarters than those who waited for the inquiries to come to them.

Garage floor epoxy continued to grow. The residential market for quality garage floor coatings in Las Vegas has been on a consistent growth trajectory and 2026 extended that trend. The shift from standard solid-color epoxy systems toward metallic and decorative flake systems has accelerated as homeowners have become more familiar with the options. Contractors who invested in learning metallic epoxy application earlier in the year were well positioned to meet that demand.

Commercial work was mixed depending on the segment. Hospitality renovation — hotels, restaurants, entertainment venues — was active, which is consistent with the Las Vegas hospitality industry's investment cycle. Retail was more cautious. The large commercial projects that drive the highest revenue quarters for contractors doing commercial work were selective in 2026.

## What Surprised Us

A few things stood out as different from what we expected coming into the year.

The pace of transition to polyaspartic topcoat systems on commercial and high-end residential floors was faster than anticipated. Contractors who had been using standard epoxy systems for years made the switch to polyaspartic topcoats in meaningful numbers, driven primarily by the return-to-service time advantage. A floor that can be walked on in hours rather than days is a genuinely different value proposition for commercial clients and the market moved toward it more quickly than the historical rate of product adoption would have suggested.

Water-based sealer technology continued to close the performance gap with solvent-based systems. Several years ago the conversation about water-based versus solvent-based sealers was relatively simple — solvent-based products outperformed water-based on most outdoor applications. The formulation improvements in premium water-based sealers have made that comparison more nuanced, and we saw more experienced contractors switching to high-quality water-based options in 2026 than in previous years.

The interest in decorative concrete among first-time customers — homeowners who had never considered it before — remained higher than the pre-2020 baseline. The home investment trend that started during the pandemic years has proven more durable than many expected. Homeowners are still prioritizing their outdoor spaces and decorative concrete keeps benefiting from that priority.

## What We're Watching Heading Into 2026

A few things we're paying attention to as we move into the next year.

**Material cost environment.** The relatively stable pricing environment of 2026 was welcome after the volatility of earlier years. The structural factors that could move prices — energy costs, transportation costs, specialty chemical feedstock — haven't been resolved, just quieted. We're heading into 2026 without expecting dramatic moves in either direction but also without assuming stability is guaranteed.

**Labor market.** The shortage of skilled concrete workers, particularly decorative concrete specialists, remained a real constraint on what contractors in this market could execute in 2026. The contractors who invested in crew training and development during the year built a competitive advantage that will pay dividends in 2026. We expect this dynamic to continue.

**Technology and tools.** The improvement in cordless tool platforms continued and the range of professional-grade cordless options for concrete prep work expanded. The trend toward cordless equipment on job sites where cord management is a constraint will continue. Contractors who haven't evaluated the current generation of cordless grinding and prep tools may find them more capable than they expect.

**Sustainability questions.** The frequency with which commercial clients are asking questions about sustainable materials and practices continued to increase in 2026. This is still more common in commercial specifications than in residential conversations, but the trend is clear. Contractors who understand the sustainability story of the products they're using — recycled content, low VOC formulations, products that extend service life and reduce replacement frequency — are better positioned for commercial work that includes sustainability criteria.

## What Stays True

Amid everything that changes year to year, a few things stay true in this business and are worth saying again.

The quality of surface preparation determines the quality of the finished work more than any other single factor. This was true in 2015, it's true in 2026, and it will be true in 2035. Every year we see the same failures caused by the same preparation shortcuts and every year the lesson is the same.

Customers — whether homeowners or commercial clients — are better informed than they used to be. The contractor who can speak knowledgeably about their product choices and application methods, who can explain why they're doing what they're doing, earns more trust and more referrals than one who can't. Product knowledge is a competitive advantage that matters more as customers become more informed.

Relationships with suppliers, with other contractors, with past customers — these relationships are the foundation of a sustainable concrete business in a local market. The contractors who have been consistently busy in Las Vegas through the ups and downs of the past several years are almost universally contractors with strong local relationships.
