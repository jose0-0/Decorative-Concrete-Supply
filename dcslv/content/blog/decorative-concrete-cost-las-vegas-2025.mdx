## What Drives Decorative Concrete Pricing

Before getting into specific project types, it helps to understand the variables that move prices up or down on any decorative concrete project.

**Substrate condition** is the most significant variable that homeowners often don't account for. A surface in poor condition with multiple cracks, an existing failing coating, oil contamination, or other issues that require extensive preparation costs significantly more to work with than a surface in good condition. Two patios of identical square footage can have meaningfully different project costs if one needs two hours of prep and the other needs eight.

**Design complexity** affects both material cost and labor time. A plain broom-finish overlay is faster to execute than a multi-color stamped pattern with border details. A single-color garage floor epoxy is faster than a metallic epoxy with flake broadcast and multiple topcoats. More complexity means more time and more product, which means more cost.

**Product quality** affects cost and longevity. Professional-grade products formulated for Las Vegas conditions cost more than contractor-grade alternatives and significantly more than consumer retail products. The difference shows up in how long the finished surface holds up before it needs attention again. Cheaper products on a Las Vegas outdoor surface in constant UV exposure need replacement sooner, which affects the true long-term cost even if the initial price is lower.

**Contractor experience and reputation** affects pricing. Contractors who do excellent decorative work and have a track record of quality jobs in this market charge more than those who are newer to decorative work or whose track record is less established. That premium is usually worth paying for complex decorative projects where the quality of execution is visible in the finished result.

**Square footage** affects cost per square foot through economies of scale. Larger projects generally have lower per-square-foot costs because mobilization, setup, and crew time is distributed over more area. A 200 square foot patio costs more per square foot than a 600 square foot patio using the same product system.

## Patio Overlays and Resurfacing

Patio overlay projects cover a wide range depending on the scope and the system used.

A basic resurfacing overlay on a sound patio — cleaning and prep, thin overlay application, and sealing — is among the more affordable decorative concrete projects on a per-square-foot basis. The work is relatively straightforward when the substrate cooperates and the execution time is predictable.

Adding color through staining or integral color to the overlay increases material cost and adds a process step. Adding texture — broom finish, knockdown, or skip trowel — adds labor time but not significant material cost. Adding stamped patterns adds both material cost and labor time and represents the higher end of the overlay pricing range.

For budgeting purposes, a realistic range for a quality patio overlay project in Las Vegas in 2025 runs from the lower end for basic resurfacing on a good substrate to the higher end for complex stamped and colored work on a substrate that needs significant preparation. Projects at the lower end of that range are possible when conditions are ideal. Projects at the higher end often involve decorative complexity, substrate challenges, or both.

## Pool Deck Resurfacing

Pool deck resurfacing is one of the most consistent project types in the Las Vegas market and pricing is reasonably predictable because the application requirements are well understood.

The primary product options for pool deck resurfacing are spray deck systems, thin resurfacing overlays, and decorative overlay systems. Each sits at a different price point with spray deck being the most economical and decorative stamped or stained overlays at the higher end.

Pool deck pricing in Las Vegas is affected by a few specific factors beyond the usual variables. Pool decks often have features that add complexity: steps, raised bond beams, waterline tile transitions, and equipment pads that require careful masking and detailing. The larger the pool deck and the more features it has, the more those details add to the project cost.

Non-slip requirement is a factor in product selection that affects cost. Pool deck systems with built-in non-slip texture or with non-slip additives cost more than standard overlay systems but are the appropriate specification for a wet surface around a pool. Cutting corners on non-slip performance to save money on a pool deck is not a trade-off worth making.

## Stamped Concrete — New Pours

Stamped concrete on a new pour — a new patio, driveway extension, or pool deck addition — is priced differently from overlay work because it involves concrete placement as well as decorative finishing.

The concrete placement cost — ready-mix, forming, and rebar — is a baseline cost that exists independent of the decorative finish. The decorative steps — color hardener, release agent, stamping, and sealing — add to that baseline.

Stamped concrete pricing varies by pattern complexity, color system, and slab size. Simple patterns with a single color combination are at the lower end of the range. Complex multi-color patterns with border details and multiple stamp configurations are at the higher end. The skill required to execute complex stamped work well is part of why the higher end of the range exists — experienced stamped concrete contractors who deliver consistent quality in Las Vegas conditions command and deserve a premium over less experienced alternatives.

## Epoxy Garage Floors

Epoxy garage floor pricing in Las Vegas spans a range that depends heavily on the specific system used and the condition of the existing floor.

A standard solid-color epoxy system on a two-car garage floor — proper mechanical prep, primer, epoxy base coat, and clear topcoat — is a reasonable project for a homeowner or a straightforward job for a contractor at the lower end of the market range.

Metallic epoxy systems, broadcast flake systems with multiple layers, and polyaspartic top coats represent the higher end. These systems involve more product, more labor, and in some cases specialized application technique. The visual results are also more dramatic — metallic epoxy floors in particular have a distinctive appearance that standard solid-color systems don't achieve.

Existing floor condition significantly affects pricing. A floor with active moisture transmission requires a moisture mitigation primer before any coating goes down. A floor with an existing coating that needs to be ground off requires more prep time. A floor with oil staining from years of vehicle use requires more aggressive degreasing. Each of these conditions adds cost that isn't visible in a per-square-foot comparison between quotes.

## What the Pricing Range Means for Getting Quotes

Understanding the range and what drives it changes how you approach the quoting process.

When you get multiple quotes for the same project and they vary significantly, the variation usually reflects one of three things: different product systems being specified, different assessments of the substrate condition and the prep required, or different contractor cost structures and margins. Asking each contractor to explain what's in their quote — specifically what product system they're using and what their prep approach includes — gives you the information to compare quotes meaningfully rather than just looking at the bottom line number.

The lowest quote is not always the best value. A quote built on minimal prep and lower-cost products will produce a result that needs attention again sooner, which affects the actual cost per year of service life. A quote built on thorough prep and quality products costs more upfront but typically produces a result that lasts longer and requires less maintenance.

For the most common project types in Las Vegas — pool deck resurfacing, patio overlays, garage floor coatings — there's a price range below which quality work simply can't be done. When a quote comes in significantly below the range that reflects the real cost of doing the work well, it's worth asking what's being left out.
