Bidding is where a lot of concrete contractors leave money on the table — or, worse, commit to jobs they lose money on. The work itself might be excellent. The craftsmanship might be the best in the market. But if the bid doesn't accurately account for what the job actually costs, the quality of the work doesn't matter for the business's financial health.

Decorative concrete is particularly susceptible to bidding problems because the variables are more complex than plain flatwork. The product systems involve more components. Prep requirements vary widely based on substrate condition. Skilled labor for decorative work costs more than general concrete labor. And the consequence of underbidding a decorative job — a callback, a repair, a customer who's unhappy with a rushed finish — has reputational costs beyond the direct financial ones.

This framework won't tell you what to charge per square foot. Those numbers are specific to your market, your overhead, and your business model. What it will do is give you a systematic approach to building bids that account for all the real costs and protect your margin.

## Start With a Thorough Site Visit

Every decorative concrete bid starts with seeing the actual job site. Photos from the customer are useful background but they're not a substitute for walking the surface yourself, probing the substrate, and understanding the specific conditions of that particular project.

What you're assessing on the site visit:

**Substrate condition.** Is the existing concrete sound? Are there cracks, delamination, previous coatings, oil contamination, or other conditions that will affect prep requirements? A surface that looks acceptable in photos may require significantly more preparation when you're standing on it and tapping it with a screwdriver handle.

**Access and logistics.** How does equipment and material get to the work area? Is there a gate that limits what can be brought through? Are there plants, finished surfaces, or other features adjacent to the work area that need protection? Is there a place to stage materials and mix product? Access and logistics have real cost implications that don't show up on a square footage calculation.

**Scope clarity.** Make sure you and the customer have the same understanding of what's included. Where exactly does the work start and stop? What happens at the transitions to adjacent surfaces? Are there steps, columns, or other features within the work area that require special handling? Scope ambiguity that gets resolved after the bid has been accepted almost always costs the contractor money.

**Project-specific conditions.** Is there a timing requirement — does the pool deck need to be done before a specific event? Is the customer doing other work concurrently that will affect job site access? Are there HOA requirements about work hours or material staging? These conditions affect scheduling and sometimes cost.

Take notes and photos on the site visit. You'll reference them when building the bid and they're documentation if questions come up later.

## Build Your Cost Components Systematically

A complete decorative concrete bid has several cost components and each needs to be calculated from the actual project conditions, not from a per-square-foot rule of thumb.

### Materials

Material cost is the most straightforward component if you do it carefully. Start with the square footage of the work area — measured from the site visit, not estimated from the customer's description. Apply the coverage rates for each product in the system you're specifying, adjusted for the actual substrate conditions.

Key adjustments that affect material quantities:

A porous or rough substrate uses more primer and more overlay material than the stated coverage rate suggests. Add 15 to 20 percent over the theoretical calculation for typical Las Vegas residential concrete. For highly porous or textured surfaces, add more.

Product systems with multiple components — primer, overlay, color, sealer — require separate quantity calculations for each component. A mistake on any one of them affects the total material cost.

For products with limited shelf life or opened containers that can't be reused on other jobs, factor in the full unit cost of any partially used container rather than the prorated cost.

Add a small buffer — five to ten percent — to the total material cost calculation. Running out of product midway through a job is a worse outcome than having a modest overage.

### Labor

Labor is where decorative concrete bids go wrong most often, usually by underestimating the time required for preparation and for the decorative finishing steps.

Build labor cost from time estimates rather than from a flat per-square-foot rate. The time required for a given job depends on the substrate condition, the product system, the complexity of the decorative work, and the crew size. A straightforward overlay on sound, clean concrete takes less time per square foot than a stamped and colored overlay on a pool deck with scattered cracks, oil stains, and adjacent landscaping that needs protection.

Separate the time estimate for each phase:

Mobilization and setup — time to travel to the site, unload equipment, stage materials, and protect adjacent surfaces. Don't forget demobilization time for cleanup, equipment loading, and site restoration.

Surface preparation — grinding, cleaning, crack repair. This is the most variable phase. A surface in poor condition can take three to four times longer to prepare than one in good condition. Base the estimate on what you saw during the site visit, not on what a typical surface looks like.

Product application — each coat, including mix time, application, and any texturing or stamping work. Stamping time per square foot is significantly more than plain overlay application time. Be honest about how long it actually takes your crew to execute stamped work at quality.

Curing and inspection time — the time spent monitoring curing conditions, inspecting the work, and addressing any issues before moving to the next coat.

Labor rate needs to reflect the fully loaded cost of your crew — wages, payroll taxes, insurance, and any benefits — not just the hourly wage. If you use subcontract labor for certain tasks, use the actual subcontract rate.

### Equipment and Overhead

Equipment cost includes depreciation on owned equipment allocated to the job, rental costs for any equipment rented specifically for the project, and consumable tooling like grinding segments and trowel blades that will be used on the job.

Overhead allocation varies by business model but every bid needs to contribute to covering the fixed costs of running the business — insurance, vehicle costs, tools and equipment maintenance, office expenses, and owner compensation. If you're not including overhead in your bids, every job you do is subsidized by whatever income you have elsewhere.

## The Substrate Condition Variable

Substrate condition deserves specific attention because it's the biggest source of bid variance in decorative concrete work and the one most often handled with a generic allowance rather than a specific assessment.

The prep work required for a pool deck with scattered surface spalling, oil staining near the equipment pad, two hairline cracks that need routing and filling, and a failing previous sealer is completely different from the prep for a sound slab that just needs light grinding and cleaning. Treating both with the same per-square-foot prep allowance means one of those jobs is significantly over- or under-priced.

When a substrate is in poor or uncertain condition, two approaches protect you: build a more conservative material and labor estimate that accounts for the worst realistic case, or include a specific allowance for additional prep in the bid with an explicit provision that allows additional charges if the substrate requires more work than expected.

The second approach requires clear communication with the customer upfront about what constitutes additional prep and what it would cost. It's a harder conversation than a fixed price but it's more honest and it protects both parties from surprises.

## Markup and Margin

The difference between your total cost and what you charge the customer is your gross margin. What margin is appropriate depends on your market, your overhead structure, and the competitive environment for the specific type of work.

What's not appropriate is bidding at cost or near cost because you need the work or because you're trying to compete with a lower bidder. Bids below your actual cost don't become profitable just because you win them. They drain resources, tie up your crew on jobs that don't contribute to the business, and set customer expectations on price that are unsustainable.

If your bid is higher than a competitor and you lose the job, that's a better outcome than winning a job that loses money. Knowing your costs well enough to bid accurately — and being willing to walk away from work that can't be done profitably at market prices — is one of the marks of a contractor who runs a sustainable business.

## Presenting the Bid

A well-prepared bid document builds customer confidence even before the job starts. It shows that you've thought through the project thoroughly and that you know what you're doing.

Include in your bid:

A clear description of the scope — exactly what work is included and what's excluded. The product system being specified — what's going into the job. The project timeline — start date, completion date, and any phases if the work is staged. Payment terms — deposit requirements and payment schedule. Any conditions that could affect scope or price, explicitly stated.

For decorative work, including examples of completed projects in similar styles and product systems gives the customer a realistic preview of the result and reduces the likelihood of expectation mismatches after the job is complete.

## After the Bid — Learning From Each Job

The most useful thing a contractor can do to improve their bidding accuracy over time is to track actual costs against estimated costs on every job. Where did the estimate hold up? Where was it significantly off? What conditions were responsible for the variance?

Actual cost data from real jobs is the most accurate input for future estimates. A contractor who tracks job profitability systematically gets better at estimating every year. One who bids by gut feel without feedback never knows where the gaps in their process are.
